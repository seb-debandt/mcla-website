## First Steps After Diagnosis

1. **Confirm your diagnosis.** Make sure your biopsy has been reviewed by a pathologist experienced in lymphoma. MCL has a specific genetic marker — the t(11;14) translocation — that should be confirmed.

2. **Seek a specialist.** MCL is rare, and treatment approaches continue to evolve. We strongly recommend consulting with a hematologist-oncologist who has specific experience treating MCL, even if your primary treatment happens closer to home.

3. **Understand your subtype.** Ask your doctor about the specific characteristics of your MCL, including the Ki-67 index (a marker of how fast cells are growing) and whether your disease appears to be the classic or indolent variant. This information helps guide treatment decisions.

4. **Complete staging tests.** Your care team will likely order additional tests — imaging (CT or PET scan), blood work, and possibly a bone marrow biopsy — to determine the stage and extent of your disease.

5. **Bring someone with you.** Having a trusted family member or friend at your appointments can help you process information and remember details.

## Questions to Ask Your Doctor

- What subtype of MCL do I have?
- What is the stage of my disease?
- What are my treatment options?
- Would you recommend a second opinion from an MCL specialist?
- Are there clinical trials I should consider?
- What can I expect from treatment — timeline, side effects, follow-up?
- How will we monitor for response and potential relapse?

## Building Your Support Network

Living with MCL is not something you need to do alone. Consider connecting with:

- A social worker or patient navigator at your treatment center
- Other MCL patients through support groups or online communities
- Caregiver support resources for your family members
- Financial counseling services if treatment costs are a concern
