Mantle cell lymphoma (MCL) is a rare type of non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL) — a cancer that begins in white blood cells called lymphocytes. MCL develops from B-lymphocytes in the outer edge (or "mantle zone") of a lymph node. It accounts for approximately 6% of all NHL cases in the United States.

## Key Facts

- **Incidence:** Approximately 4,000 new cases are diagnosed each year in the United States.
- **Age:** The median age at diagnosis is 68 years old.
- **Gender:** MCL affects men approximately three times more often than women.
- **Genetic marker:** MCL is characterized by a chromosomal translocation — t(11;14) — that leads to overproduction of the cyclin D1 protein, which drives abnormal cell growth.

## Signs and Symptoms

MCL symptoms can vary, but common signs include:

- Swollen lymph nodes (often painless) in the neck, armpit, or groin
- Fatigue and weakness
- Unexplained weight loss
- Night sweats
- Fever without infection
- Loss of appetite or abdominal discomfort

Some patients may have no symptoms at diagnosis, with MCL discovered during routine blood work or examinations.

## How MCL Is Diagnosed

MCL is typically diagnosed through:

- **Lymph node biopsy:** A tissue sample is examined for cancer cells.
- **Immunophenotyping:** Tests identify the specific type of lymphoma cells.
- **Genetic testing:** The t(11;14) translocation and cyclin D1 overexpression confirm the diagnosis.
- **Imaging:** CT scans, PET scans, and bone marrow biopsies help determine the stage and extent of disease.

## What Makes MCL Different

MCL is unique among lymphomas because it can behave in different ways. Some forms are more aggressive and require immediate treatment, while others (sometimes called indolent or leukemic non-nodal MCL) may grow more slowly and can sometimes be monitored before treatment is needed.

This variability makes it especially important to work with an oncologist who has experience treating MCL and can help determine the best approach for your specific situation.
